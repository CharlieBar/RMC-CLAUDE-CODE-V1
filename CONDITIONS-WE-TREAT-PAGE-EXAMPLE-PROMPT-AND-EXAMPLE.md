**H2: What is Sciatica?**

Sciatica is not actually a condition itself, but rather a symptom of an underlying problem affecting the sciatic nerve‚Äîthe largest nerve in your body. The sciatic nerve runs from your lower back, through your hips and buttocks, and down each leg. When this nerve becomes compressed or irritated, it causes the characteristic pain, numbness, and tingling known as sciatica.

Sciatica affects approximately 40% of people at some point in their lives, with the highest incidence occurring in people between ages 30 and 50. While it can be extremely painful, the good news is that most cases respond well to non-surgical treatment, and severe cases requiring surgery are rare.

The pain of sciatica can significantly impact your quality of life, making it difficult to:
- Sit for extended periods (driving, working at a desk)
- Stand or walk for long durations

**H2: What Causes Sciatica?**

Sciatica occurs when the sciatic nerve becomes compressed, irritated, or inflamed. Understanding the underlying cause is essential for effective treatment.

**Most Common Causes:**

**Herniated Disc** (Most Common Cause)
When the soft inner material of a spinal disc pushes through its outer layer, it can compress the sciatic nerve root. This accounts for approximately 90% of sciatica cases. [Learn more about herniated disc treatment ‚Üí](link-to-herniated-disc-page)

**Spinal Stenosis**
Narrowing of the spinal canal can put pressure on the sciatic nerve, particularly common in people over 60. [Learn more about spinal stenosis ‚Üí](link-to-spinal-stenosis-page)

**Degenerative Disc Disease**
Age-related wear and tear on spinal discs can lead to nerve compression and sciatica symptoms. [Learn more about degenerative disc disease ‚Üí](link-to-degenerative-disc-page)

**Spondylolisthesis**
When one vertebra slips forward over another, it can pinch the sciatic nerve.

**Piriformis Syndrome**
The piriformis muscle in the buttock can spasm and compress the sciatic nerve.

**Spinal Tumors or Infections** (Rare)
Though uncommon, tumors or infections can compress the sciatic nerve.

**Other Contributing Factors:**
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (pressure from the growing uterus)
‚Ä¢ Obesity (extra weight increases spinal stress)
‚Ä¢ Prolonged sitting (especially with poor posture)
‚Ä¢ Diabetes (nerve damage from high blood sugar)

**Risk Factors:**
- Age (most common between 30-50)

**H2: Frequently Asked Questions About Sciatica**

**How long does sciatica last?**
Acute sciatica typically lasts 4-6 weeks with proper treatment. However, without treatment, it can become chronic and last months or years. Early intervention significantly improves recovery time.

**Can sciatica go away on its own?**
Some mild cases may resolve without treatment in 4-8 weeks. However, waiting carries risks: symptoms may worsen, nerve damage may occur, and chronic pain may develop. It's best to seek evaluation and treatment early.

**What's the best sleeping position for sciatica?**
Side sleeping with a pillow between your knees, or back sleeping with a pillow under your knees, typically reduces nerve pressure. Avoid stomach sleeping. Invest in a supportive mattress.

**Can I exercise with sciatica?**
Gentle exercise like walking and specific stretches can help. Avoid high-impact activities, heavy lifting, and exercises that worsen pain. Our physical therapists will design a safe exercise program for you.

**How effective are epidural steroid injections for sciatica?**
Epidural injections have a 70-90% success rate for sciatica caused by herniated discs or spinal stenosis. Relief can last weeks to months, and the procedure can be repeated if needed.

**Will I need surgery for my sciatica?**
Only about 10-20% of sciatica patients require surgery. We exhaust all conservative and interventional treatment options first. Surgery is reserved for severe cases or those not responding to other treatments.

**How much does sciatica treatment cost?**
Costs vary based on treatment type and your insurance coverage. Most insurance plans cover sciatica treatment. We'll verify your coverage and discuss costs before starting treatment. For uninsured patients, we offer transparent, affordable pricing.

**Can sciatica cause permanent damage?**
If left untreated, severe sciatica can cause permanent nerve damage, resulting in chronic pain, weakness, or loss of function. This is rare but emphasizes the importance of early treatment.

**What's the difference between sciatica and general back pain?**
Sciatica specifically involves the sciatic nerve and causes radiating pain down the leg, often with numbness or tingling. General back pain is typically localized to the back without leg symptoms.

**Can physical therapy alone cure sciatica?**
Many patients achieve full recovery with physical therapy alone, especially when started early. However, some cases require additional treatments like injections or medications. We'll monitor your progress and adjust treatment as needed.

**Is sciatica hereditary?**
While sciatica itself isn't inherited, factors that increase risk (like degenerative disc disease or spinal structure) can run in families.

**Can stress cause sciatica?**
Stress doesn't directly cause sciatica, but it can increase muscle tension and inflammation, potentially triggering or worsening symptoms. Stress management is part of comprehensive treatment.

**H2: Related Conditions**

Sciatica is often confused with or related to other spinal conditions:

**[Herniated Disc](link-to-herniated-disc-page)**
The most common cause of sciatica. When disc material compresses the sciatic nerve root, it causes radiating leg pain.

**[Spinal Stenosis](link-to-spinal-stenosis-page)**
Narrowing of the spinal canal can compress the sciatic nerve, particularly common in older adults. Symptoms include leg pain with walking.

**[Piriformis Syndrome](link-to-piriformis-syndrome-page)**
The piriformis muscle in the buttock can irritate the sciatic nerve, causing similar symptoms to disc-related sciatica but with different treatment needs.

**[Degenerative Disc Disease](link-to-degenerative-disc-page)**
Age-related disc wear can lead to nerve compression and sciatica symptoms. Often responds well to conservative treatment.

**[Sacroiliac Joint Dysfunction](link-to-si-joint-page)**
SI joint problems can cause buttock and leg pain that mimics sciatica but requires different treatment approaches.
