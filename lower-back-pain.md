## Understanding Work-Related Lower Back Pain

Lower back pain is the most common work-related injury in the United States, affecting millions of workers every year. Whether caused by a single incident like lifting a heavy box or developing gradually from repetitive strain, work-related back pain can be debilitating and prevent you from doing your job.

At Rand Medical Center, we specialize in diagnosing and treating lower back injuries sustained on the job. We understand that injured workers need fast, effective treatment to get back to work and resume their normal lives.

## Common Causes of Workplace Back Injuries

**Acute Injuries (Sudden Onset):**
- Lifting objects that are too heavy
- Lifting while twisting or in awkward positions
- Sudden falls or impacts
- Slipping and catching yourself
- Being struck by falling objects

**Chronic Injuries (Gradual Onset):**
- Years of improper lifting technique
- Repetitive bending and reaching
- Prolonged sitting with poor posture
- Standing on hard surfaces without support
- Vibration from vehicles or machinery
- Poor workplace ergonomics

Many back injuries that seem sudden actually developed over time. That "final straw" moment when you lift something and feel your back give out often happens because tissues were already weakened from accumulated stress.

## Types of Lower Back Injuries We Treat

**Muscle Strains and Sprains:**
The most common type of back injury occurs when muscles or ligaments are overstretched or torn. This causes pain, spasm, and stiffness.

**Herniated or Bulging Discs:**
The cushioning discs between vertebrae can rupture or bulge out, pressing on nearby nerves and causing severe pain that may radiate down the leg (sciatica).

**Facet Joint Injuries:**
Small joints in the spine can become inflamed or arthritic from wear and tear or sudden trauma.

**Spinal Stenosis:**
Narrowing of the spinal canal that puts pressure on nerves, often developing from years of heavy work.

**Sacroiliac Joint Dysfunction:**
Problems with the joint connecting your spine to your pelvis, often from falls or repetitive stress.

## Recognizing Serious Back Injuries

Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:
- Severe pain that doesn't improve with rest
- Numbness or weakness in legs
- Loss of bowel or bladder control
- Pain radiating down both legs
- Fever along with back pain
- Pain after a significant fall or accident

For most work injuries, you should see a doctor within 24-48 hours to document the injury and begin treatment.

## Injection Therapy for Back Pain

When conservative treatment alone isn't enough, targeted injections can provide significant relief:

**Epidural Steroid Injections:**
Deliver powerful anti-inflammatory medication directly around inflamed nerve roots, providing relief from sciatica and disc-related pain.

**Facet Joint Injections:**
Target specific spinal joints to reduce arthritis pain and inflammation.

**Trigger Point Injections:**
Release painful muscle knots and spasms that develop after injury.

**SI Joint Injections:**
Address pain in the sacroiliac joint at the base of the spine.

## Preventing Future Back Injuries

Once you've recovered, protecting your back from future injury is crucial:

**Proper Lifting Technique:**
1. Stand close to the object
2. Bend at your knees, not your waist
3. Keep the load close to your body
4. Lift with your legs, not your back
5. Avoid twisting while lifting
6. Ask for help with heavy or awkward items

**Workplace Modifications:**
- Adjust workstation height and layout
- Use mechanical lifting aids when available
- Take microbreaks to stretch
- Maintain good posture
- Wear supportive footwear
- Request ergonomic equipment

**Personal Prevention:**
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Exercise regularly to keep muscles strong
- Stretch daily, especially before work
- Stop smoking (impairs disc health)
- Manage stress that causes muscle tension
