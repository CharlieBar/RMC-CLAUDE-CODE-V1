## Understanding Work-Related Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) is one of the most common work-related injuries affecting office workers, assembly line employees, and anyone performing repetitive hand motions. When the median nerve becomes compressed as it passes through the narrow carpal tunnel in your wrist, it causes numbness, tingling, pain, and weakness in your hand.

At Rand Medical Center, we specialize in diagnosing and treating work-related carpal tunnel syndrome. Early intervention is key - the sooner you address symptoms, the better your chances of avoiding surgery and permanent nerve damage.

## What Causes Carpal Tunnel Syndrome at Work?

The carpal tunnel is a narrow passageway in your wrist made up of bones and ligaments. The median nerve and nine tendons pass through this tunnel to reach your hand. When repetitive motions cause inflammation and swelling in this confined space, pressure builds on the median nerve, leading to carpal tunnel syndrome.

**Common Workplace Activities That Cause CTS:**

**Computer Work:**
- Hours of typing on keyboards
- Constant mouse clicking and movement
- Wrist resting on hard desk surfaces
- Poor monitor and keyboard positioning

**Manual Labor:**
- Gripping vibrating power tools
- Repetitive assembly line work
- Using hand tools for extended periods
- Forceful gripping or pinching motions

**Retail and Service:**
- Scanning items at checkout
- Operating cash registers
- Repetitive product handling
- Extended point-of-sale system use

**Healthcare:**
- Dental hygienists' repetitive hand positions
- Surgical instrument use
- Patient lifting and positioning
- Medical charting and computer work

## Recognizing the Signs

Carpal tunnel syndrome typically develops gradually. You might initially dismiss symptoms as your hand "falling asleep," but CTS has distinct characteristics:

**Early Warning Signs:**
- Occasional tingling in thumb, index, and middle fingers
- Mild numbness that comes and goes
- Symptoms worse at night or first thing in morning
- Shaking your hand provides temporary relief

**Progressive Symptoms:**
- Constant numbness and tingling
- Pain radiating up the forearm
- Weakness in grip strength
- Dropping objects you're trying to hold
- Difficulty with buttons, zippers, and small objects

**Severe Symptoms:**
- Persistent numbness even at rest
- Visible muscle wasting at base of thumb
- Inability to distinguish hot from cold by touch
- Severe pain disrupting sleep

Don't wait until symptoms become severe. Early treatment prevents permanent nerve damage and often avoids the need for surgery.

## Diagnosis at Rand Medical Center

**Clinical Examination:**
Our specialists perform specific tests to diagnose carpal tunnel syndrome:

- **Tinel's Sign**: Tapping over the median nerve at your wrist - tingling in fingers indicates nerve compression
- **Phalen's Test**: Holding wrists in flexed position - numbness within 60 seconds suggests CTS
- **Grip and Pinch Strength**: Measuring weakness compared to unaffected hand
- **Sensation Testing**: Evaluating numbness distribution in fingers

**Nerve Conduction Studies:**
For confirmation and severity assessment, we may perform electrodiagnostic testing:
- Measures electrical signals through the median nerve
- Determines exact location and degree of compression
- Helps rule out other nerve problems
- Guides treatment decisions

## Conservative Treatment Options

Most cases of carpal tunnel syndrome respond well to non-surgical treatment, especially when caught early.

### 1. Wrist Splinting

Wearing a splint keeps your wrist in a neutral position, reducing pressure on the median nerve:
- Worn primarily at night when symptoms worsen
- May also be worn during work activities
- Custom-fitted or over-the-counter options
- Often provides relief within 2-4 weeks

### 2. Corticosteroid Injections

Targeted injections deliver powerful anti-inflammatory medication directly into the carpal tunnel:
- Reduces swelling around the nerve
- Provides relief lasting weeks to months
- Can be repeated if symptoms return
- Helps determine if surgery might be beneficial
- Covered by workers' compensation

### 3. Physical Therapy

Specialized hand therapy helps reduce symptoms and prevent recurrence:
- **Nerve gliding exercises** to help the median nerve move freely
- **Tendon gliding** to reduce inflammation
- **Strengthening exercises** for hand and forearm muscles
- **Stretching** to improve flexibility
- **Ergonomic training** to modify work techniques

### 4. Activity Modification and Ergonomics

Adjusting how you work can dramatically reduce symptoms:
- **Keyboard position**: Keep wrists neutral, not bent up or down
- **Mouse placement**: Position close to keyboard to avoid reaching
- **Chair height**: Allow forearms parallel to floor
- **Wrist rests**: Use padded support at keyboard
- **Take breaks**: 5-minute break every hour for hand exercises
- **Tool selection**: Use tools with larger, padded grips

### 5. Anti-inflammatory Medications

NSAIDs like ibuprofen can reduce swelling and pain, particularly in early stages.

## When Surgery is Necessary

If conservative treatments don't provide adequate relief after 3-6 months, or if you have severe symptoms with muscle wasting, surgery may be recommended.

**Carpal Tunnel Release Surgery:**
- Cuts the ligament pressing on the median nerve
- Can be done through open or endoscopic technique
- Outpatient procedure with local anesthesia
- Recovery takes 6-12 weeks
- Success rate over 90%
- Covered by workers' compensation

At Rand Medical Center, we exhaust all conservative options before recommending surgery, and we coordinate surgical referrals when needed.

## Preventing Carpal Tunnel Recurrence

After successful treatment, protecting your hands and wrists prevents the problem from returning:

**Workplace Modifications:**
- Ergonomic keyboard and mouse
- Adjustable chair and desk height
- Document holder at eye level
- Phone headset instead of cradling handset
- Anti-fatigue mats for standing work
- Padded, ergonomic tool handles

**Daily Exercises:**
Perform these stretches throughout your workday:

1. **Wrist flexion/extension**: Gently bend wrist up and down, hold 5 seconds each
2. **Nerve glides**: Make "OK" sign, straighten fingers, repeat 10 times
3. **Fist pumps**: Make tight fist, release fully, repeat 10 times
4. **Prayer stretch**: Press palms together, lower hands until stretch felt

**Work Habits:**
- Take 5-minute break every hour
- Alternate tasks to vary hand movements
- Avoid gripping tools or objects too tightly
- Keep wrists neutral, not bent
- Stay warm - cold increases stiffness

## Don't Ignore the Warning Signs

Many workers try to "tough it out" with carpal tunnel symptoms, hoping they'll go away on their own. Unfortunately, untreated CTS typically worsens over time and can lead to:
- Permanent nerve damage
- Irreversible muscle wasting
- Chronic pain
- Loss of hand function
- Need for more invasive treatment

Early treatment is more effective, less invasive, and gets you back to work faster.
